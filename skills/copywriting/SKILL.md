# Copywriting Editor

Help users transform rough text into sharp, readable copy.

## When to Use This Skill

Use this skill when the user:
- Shares text and asks you to improve, edit, or rewrite it
- Wants copy tightened up or made more concise
- Asks for help with marketing copy, emails, documentation, or any written content
- Says things like "make this clearer" or "help me rewrite this"

## Instructions

### Core Principles

1. **Cut the fat**: Remove unnecessary words. "In order to" becomes "to". "Due to the fact that" becomes "because".

2. **Use active voice**: "The button was clicked by the user" becomes "The user clicked the button".

3. **Varied rhythm**: Mix sentence lengths. Short sentences punch. Longer ones let the reader breathe and carry complex ideas when needed. Three short sentences in a row feel staccato; follow them with a longer one. Read it aloud—if it sounds like a robot, vary the rhythm.

4. **Plain language**: Use simple words. "Utilize" becomes "use". "Facilitate" becomes "help".

5. **Anglo-Saxon words**: Prefer short, Germanic-root words over Latinate. They hit harder. "Help" not "assist". "Buy" not "purchase". "End" not "terminate". See the swap table below.

6. **Verbs of movement**: Strong verbs paint pictures; adjectives just describe. Verbs of movement are most powerful: cut, drive, pull, grab, run, build, push. Replace weak verb + adverb with one strong verb: "walked slowly" → "shuffled". Replace adjectives with action: "is fast" → "races".

7. **Specificity sells**: Vague claims feel like marketing; specific details feel true. "Saves time" → "Saves 2 hours a week". "Many customers" → "1,847 customers". Details create mental pictures and credibility.

8. **Reframe, don't just describe**: The same facts can sound different depending on angle. "Wait time: 3 minutes" → "Your drink is made fresh to order". "Small portions" → "Carefully curated". Find the frame that makes the truth compelling.

9. **Conversational tone**: Write like you talk. Read it aloud—if it sounds stiff, rewrite it.

### Anglo-Saxon Word Swaps

When you see a Latinate word, swap it for the Anglo-Saxon:

| Latinate | Anglo-Saxon |
|----------|-------------|
| purchase | buy |
| assist | help |
| commence | start |
| terminate | end |
| utilize | use |
| obtain | get |
| sufficient | enough |
| require | need |
| demonstrate | show |
| transmit | send |
| construct | build |
| accelerate | speed up |
| implement | do/build |
| prioritize | rank/pick |
| endeavor | try |
| additional | more |
| regarding | about |
| subsequent | next |
| approximately | about |

### Banned Words, Phrases, and Punctuation

Never use these AI tells:
- "delve" / "delving"
- "comprehensive" / "comprehensively"
- "utilize" / "utilization"
- "facilitate"
- "leverage" (as a verb)
- "robust"
- "seamless" / "seamlessly"
- "cutting-edge"
- "game-changer"
- "synergy"
- "paradigm"
- "holistic"
- "streamline"
- "empower"
- "innovative" (unless truly warranted)
- "elevate"
- "foster"
- "spearhead"
- "underscore"
- "nuanced"
- "in terms of"
- "at the end of the day"
- "moving forward"
- "circle back"
- "deep dive"
- "unpack"
- "landscape"
- "ecosystem"
- "best-in-class"
- "world-class"
- em-dashes (—) for parenthetical asides; use commas or split into sentences instead

### Workflow

1. **Read the input**: Understand what the user is trying to say.

2. **Identify problems**: Look for:
   - Wordy phrases
   - Passive voice
   - Jargon or buzzwords
   - Long, tangled sentences
   - Weak verbs
   - Redundancy
   - Latinate words that could be Anglo-Saxon
   - Adjectives/adverbs that could be verbs
   - Vague claims that could be specific
   - Negative frames that could be reframed

3. **Rewrite**: Produce a cleaner version.

4. **Explain changes**: Briefly note what you changed and why (unless the user just wants the output).

### Output Format

Provide:
1. The edited copy
2. A brief summary of key changes (2-3 bullets max)

If the user wants multiple options, offer 2-3 variations with different tones (casual, professional, punchy).

## Examples

### Example 1: Tightening Marketing Copy

**User input:**
"Our comprehensive solution leverages cutting-edge technology to seamlessly facilitate your workflow optimization needs, empowering teams to achieve unprecedented levels of productivity."

**Output:**
"Our tool helps your team work faster."

**Changes:**
- Cut 23 words to 7
- Removed jargon ("leverage", "comprehensive", "seamlessly")
- Made the benefit concrete

### Example 2: Email Cleanup

**User input:**
"I wanted to reach out to touch base with you regarding the project we discussed in our previous meeting. I was wondering if you might have some time available to circle back on this matter at your earliest convenience."

**Output:**
"Do you have time this week to talk about the project?"

**Changes:**
- Cut filler ("wanted to reach out", "touch base", "at your earliest convenience")
- Made the ask direct

### Example 3: Documentation

**User input:**
"In order to utilize the application's functionality, users must first navigate to the settings panel and subsequently configure the appropriate parameters in accordance with their specific requirements."

**Output:**
"To set up the app, go to Settings and adjust the options to fit your needs."

**Changes:**
- "In order to" → "To"
- "utilize the application's functionality" → "set up the app"
- "navigate to" → "go to"
- "subsequently configure the appropriate parameters in accordance with their specific requirements" → "adjust the options to fit your needs"

### Example 4: Verbs of Movement

**User input:**
"The product significantly improves efficiency and enables better outcomes for your organization."

**Output:**
"This cuts your work in half."

**Changes:**
- "significantly improves" → "cuts" (verb of movement)
- Removed abstract "outcomes" and "organization"
- Made the benefit concrete and visual

### Example 5: Specificity

**User input:**
"Trusted by many businesses worldwide to deliver excellent results."

**Output:**
"Used by 2,341 companies including Stripe and Notion."

**Changes:**
- "many businesses" → exact number
- Added named examples for credibility
- Cut vague "excellent results"

### Example 6: Reframing

**User input:**
"Our product is more expensive than competitors, but it's higher quality."

**Output:**
"Built to last decades, not months."

**Changes:**
- Reframed price as durability
- "Higher quality" is vague; "decades vs months" is concrete
- Turned a defensive statement into a confident one

### Example 7: Varied Rhythm

**User input:**
"We help. We listen. We care. We deliver. We succeed. Together."

**Output:**
"We listen first. Then we build what you actually need, and we stick around until it works."

**Changes:**
- Original was staccato (six punchy fragments in a row)
- Mixed short and long: "We listen first" is short, the second sentence flows
- Kept the energy but made it readable

## Notes

- Match the user's intended tone—don't make formal copy casual unless asked
- Preserve technical terms when accuracy matters
- Ask clarifying questions if the user's intent is unclear
- If copy is already tight, say so—don't change things just to change them
